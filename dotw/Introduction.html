Introduction

What is Dungeons and Dragons?

Dungeons and Dragons, also known as D&D or DND, is a collaborative storytelling, roleplaying, and combat game. You, the player, will build your own character to take part in an adventure that is led by your Dungeon Master (DM).

Where do I start?

Right here! We teach you about Dungeons and Dragons 5th edition. If you don’t know what edition your DM is using, be sure to ask!

In what order should I build my character?

We recommend you begin by reading through Hank's section first. It's important that you understand these little pieces of the character sheet before you begin, because that's where a lot of the nuance for race and class comes in.

If you want to understand all of the character sheet before you go into making your character, you should read Lilly's section next. If you want to dive right into the roleplaying aspect, we would recommend you look into Margot's section afterwards.

Are you going to teach me the right way to character build?

We give you a lot of recommendations to help your quality of life and prevent you from future grief. We also inform you on the basics of the game. However, there is no “right” way to build a character in DND. It’s still important that you’re thinking about how to have fun! If you really want to be an elf just because you like elves, and you want to be a barbarian just because you like barbarians, then you should do it!

How do I know my character’s attribute values?

You can either roll them or use the “point buy” system to choose your own statistics.

If you are rolling, you can either use physical dice or a virtual dice roller.For each attribute, roll a d6 four times and add the highest three results together.

Point buy is a complicated system, so even experienced players usually use a point buy calculator. This calculator also includes your racial bonus, so make sure you choose a race before finalizing your attribute scores.

What is a d6 (or d8, d12, d20, etc.)?

These terms are shorthand for different dice. The number following the “d” is the highest value you can get from rolling that die. A d6 is the standard cube die you have probably used in other board games!
